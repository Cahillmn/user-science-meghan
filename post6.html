Accessibility Test

An accessibility test of the Thymes website

Posted by Meghan Cahill on May 9, 2020

Thymes is an independent company located in Minneapolis, Minnesota that produces and sells handcrafted bath/body and home fragrance products. Their website, http://www.thymes.com, provides information on available products that can be purchased online. The intended audience of this site are people who are interested in purchasing and learning more about these products.

This week I conducted an accessibility test of this site and will provide a summary of my findings.

Tools and standards used to evaluate the site

I focused on the WCAG 2.1 guidelines since this is not a federally funded site and therefore not subject to section 508. I looked at three factors to determine the accessibility of the website.

  • Whether the website can be interpreted by the user if they have impairments such as limited sight or other disabilities that would affect their ability to understand information included in the site.
  • Whether a user could operate the website with disabilities like motor impairments.
  • Is the website predictable and set up in a way to correct mistakes?

I evaluated the website by manually reviewing it and using Wave and Lighthouse.

Findings and Results

Using Lighthouse I evaluated the accessibility of the site.

Overall it gave the website a score of 80/100 indicating several issues:

  • Background and foreground colors do not have a sufficient contrast ratio
  • Form elements do not have associated labels
  • The browser zoom has been turned off
  • Links do not have a discernible name

I also reviewed the site using WAVE and found similar results. The one additional thing identified in WAVE was the use of unordered lists in their dropdown menus.

Issues and possible solutions

Background and foreground colors do not have a sufficient contrast ratio

The site uses very muted colors likely to create a relaxing experience when viewing it. This makes sense when it comes to the type of product they are selling.

It’s nice for those who can see but for those who are visually impaired, the lack of contrast can make it difficult for users to use the site. The site tries to fix this by using contrast layers behind the light grey text. On some pages, the image is so light that the white contrast layer blends into the page and you lose the contrast.

One possible solution to this would be to put a border around the contrast layer.

Form elements do not have associated labels

The site handles form labels differently in different places. For example, on the form to sign up for an account or to order goods it uses form labels. In other areas such as the search box and signing up for emails, they have just used placeholders instead of labels. They use light grey text on the placeholder which has very poor contrast and is difficult for the visually impaired.

Individuals with cognitive or motor impairments can have difficulty with placeholders because depending on how they are coded, they can act differently. For example, sometimes when you click in the field the placeholder disappears. Other times when you click in the field you have to delete the placeholder to add your information.

Lastly, not all screen readers can read placeholder text aloud making it difficult for blind or visually impaired people to know it is there. Without a label they won’t know what to put in a field.

The solution here is to include labels in all form fields and make the lines and text darker for more contrast.

The browser zoom has been turned off

Based on Lighthouse, the ability to zoom and enlarge the page has been turned off. Many visually impaired people rely on zoom to be able to read the contents of a site. This site uses small text which makes it really important to be able to zoom as needed.

The obvious solution would be to turn zoom on.

Links do not have a discernible name

In both WAVE and Lighthouse this was identified as a low priority issue. For example, the following href format is commonly used on the site. The name of the image file is the same as the alternative text. This could be a problem for users of screen readers because they wouldn’t have any more information about the image than what’s already in the image name.

On this website I don’t think it’s a big issue because both the image and the alternative text describe the product and gives the user enough information about where they will go when they click the link.

Use of unordered lists

Since the list of products is in alphabetical order, it might make more sense to use an ordered list instead of an unordered list. I navigated through the site with keyboard commands and didn’t find tabbing through the list to be a problem. The bigger problem was getting to the list from the main menu because when I hit return to open the list I still had to navigate through the utilities and main menu of the page to get to the first item of the list. Once I was in the list, tabbing took me from one item to the next alphabetically.
